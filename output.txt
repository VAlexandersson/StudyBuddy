In conclusion, the two main types of starch, amylose and amylopectin, differ in their structure, with amylose being a straight chain and amylopectin being a branched chain. This difference in structure has important implications for the properties and functions of these two types of starch.

Secondly, fluoride prevents demineralization of teeth by making them more resistant to acid attacks. This is achieved by increasing the tooth's ability to remineralize, or rebuild, its enamel structure. By strengthening the tooth's enamel, fluoride creates a barrier against acid erosion, making it more difficult for bacteria to penetrate and cause decay.

Lastly, fluoride enhances remineralization of destroyed enamel, which is a critical step in reversing tooth decay. By promoting the remineralization process, fluoride helps to repair and restore damaged enamel, reducing the risk of further decay and promoting overall oral health.

In summary, fluoride's three mechanisms of action – blocking acid formation, preventing demineralization, and enhancing remineralization – make it an effective agent in combating tooth decay and promoting oral health.
